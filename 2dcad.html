『 ☆ Raster With Gimp ☆ 』
Rasterizing refers to the process of converting vector graphics or text into a raster image composed of pixels. GIMP, which stands for GNU Image Manipulation Program, is a popular open-source software that provides a wide range of tools for image editing, including rasterising.
    Here's a step-by-step guide on how to achieve this:

  1. Open GIMP and load the image you want to work with.
    Here is the picture I will be using:

  2. Duplicate the original image layer. Right-click on the layer in the Layers panel and select "Duplicate Layer." This duplication ensures that you have a backup of the original image.
  3. Select the newly duplicated layer in the Layers panel.
  4. To rasterize the layer, go to the "Layer" menu at the top of the GIMP interface.
  5. From the dropdown menu, choose "Rasterize" and select "Layer."
  6. By rasterizing the layer, you convert it into a pixel-based format, allowing you to apply various editing techniques specific to raster images.

    7. Now we can start removing the background of the picture:

  7. Select the "Path Tool" from the toolbox on the left-hand side (shortcut: B).
  8. Start creating a path around the object by clicking and adding anchor points along the edges. Be sure to closely follow the outline of the object.

  9. To create curved or smooth lines, you can click and drag the anchor points to adjust the path handles.
  10. Continue adding anchor points until you complete the path around the entire object.
  11. Once you've closed the path by connecting the last anchor point to the first one, right-click anywhere on the path and choose "Path to Selection" from the context menu. This converts the path into a selection.

    12. Now, with the object selected, you can remove the background:

  12. Invert the selection by going to the "Select" menu at the top of the GIMP interface and choosing "Invert" (shortcut: Ctrl + I or Command + I on Mac). This selects the background instead of the object.
  13. Press the "Delete" or "Backspace" key on your keyboard to remove the selected background pixels.

    14. You can now export the image and you are done!
『 ☆ Vector editing with Inkscape ☆ 』
Rasterizing refers to the process of converting vector graphics or text into a raster image composed of pixels. GIMP, which stands for GNU Image Manipulation Program, is a popular open-source software that provides a wide range of tools for image editing, including rasterising.
    Here's a step-by-step guide on how to achieve this:

  1. Import the design: To trace your design, go to "File" and choose "Import" to bring your image into Inkscape. Select the image file and click "Open."
    We will be using this super blurry picture of a sakura flower to fully demonstrate how vector editing can turn it into a scalable and clear image!

  2. Lock the image layer: After importing the design, it's a good idea to lock the image layer to prevent accidentally moving it. Select the image and click the "Lock" icon in the "Layers" panel on the right side.
  3. Create a new layer: To separate the traced artwork from the original image, create a new layer. Click the "New Layer" icon at the bottom of the "Layers" panel.
  4. Trace the design: Select the "Bezier Curve" tool from the left toolbar (or press "B" on your keyboard). Start tracing the design by clicking on different points along the edges of the design. Use as few anchor points as possible to keep the design clean and smooth. Continue tracing until you've outlined the entire design.

  5. Edit the curves: After tracing, you can adjust and refine the curves. Select the "Edit Paths by Nodes" tool (or press "N" on your keyboard) from the left toolbar. Click on the anchor points to adjust their positions and manipulate the handles to control the curves. Aim for clean lines and curves that match the original design.
  6. Fill and stroke: Once you're satisfied with the tracing, you can add fill and stroke to your vector artwork. Select the traced design, choose a fill color from the toolbar, and adjust the stroke color, thickness, and style to your liking.

  7. Group and organize: To keep your logo design organized, select all the elements (traced design and text) and group or layer them together. Right-click and choose "Group" or use the shortcut "Ctrl+G" (or "Cmd+G" on Mac). You can also use the "Layers" panel to arrange the different elements in separate layers.

  8. Save your logo: Once you've finished creating your logo, save it in a vector format like SVG (Scalable Vector Graphics). Go to "File" and select "Save As." Choose the appropriate file format and save your logo to your desired location.

  9. And you're done! The once blurry image is not a proffesional looking scalable vector icon :3
