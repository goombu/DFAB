『 ☆ What is 3D Printing? ☆ 』

What is 3D Printing?

3D printing is also known as Additive Manufacturing, a process of creating three-dimensional objects by building them layer by layer using a computer-controlled machine (CNC).

What are the standard types of Manufacturing?

Formative: A process that makes use of stresses like compression, tension, shear, etc., to cause deformation of a material into a desired shape.

Subtractive: A process that uses computer-controlled machining to create 3D products by removing material, layer by layer starting with a solid block of material.

Material removal processes that start with solid blocks, bars, rods of plastic, metal, or other materials that are shaped by removing material through cutting, boring, drilling, and grinding.

Additive: A process that uses computer-controlled machining to create 3D products by adding material, layer by layer till the 3D shape is formed.
『 ☆ 3D Printing Workflow ☆ 』

The process of 3D printing typically involves the following steps:

  1. Designing the Model: A digital 3D model of the object is created using computer-aided design (CAD) software or obtained from a 3D scanner. The model defines the shape, size, and geometry of the object.

  2. Slicing: The 3D model is sliced into multiple thin horizontal layers using specialized software. Each layer is a cross-section of the final object. This information (usually in CNC g-code) will then be fed into the system for fabrication.

  3. Printing: The 3D printer reads the sliced model and starts the printing process. It uses various materials, such as plastics, metals, ceramics, or even living cells, depending on the printer's capabilities and the desired end product. The printer deposits or solidifies the material layer by layer, following the instructions from the sliced model.

  4. Post-Processing: Once the printing is complete, the object may require some post-processing steps, such as removing support structures, sanding, polishing, or painting, to achieve the desired finish and functionality.
『 ☆ Types of 3D Printing ☆ 』

What are the most common 3D Printing Techniques?

There are actually many types of 3D printing but it all boils down to 3 mainstream techniques:

  1. Fused Deposition Modelling (FDM)/Fused Filament Fabrication (FFF): FDM is the most widely used 3D printing technique. It involves heat melting a thermoplastic filament and depositing it layer by layer to create the object. The printer extrudes the molten material through a nozzle and moves it along a predefined path.

  2. Stereolithography Apparatus (SLA): SLA uses a liquid resin that is solidified layer by layer using a UV laser or other light sources. The UV light selectively cures the resin, creating the desired shape. SLA is known for its high level of detail and smooth surface finish.

  3. Selective Laser Sintering (SLS): SLS employs a high-powered laser that selectively fuses powdered materials, such as plastics, metals, or ceramics, layer by layer. The laser sinters the powder particles, bonding them together to form the object. SLS does not require support structures, as the unsintered powder acts as temporary support during printing.
『 ☆ 3D Printing Parameters ☆ 』

Layer Height or Thickness: Determines resolution and layer thickness. Smaller heights offer finer details but may increase printing time.

Printing Speed: Specifies speed. Faster speeds reduce printing time but may compromise quality.

Printing Temperature: Material-specific temperature for melting. Different materials require different temperatures for optimal results.

Infill Density: Refers to internal structure. Density determines material fill percentage. Higher density for stronger objects, lower density for reduced material usage and time.

Support Structures: Generated for overhangs/complex parts. Prevents sagging/collapse during printing. Includes density, pattern, and removal ease.

Plate Adhesion (Skirt, Raft or Brim): Additional structures for bed adhesion and prevention of warping. Raft extends beyond object's footprint, brim is a thin layer surrounding the object. Skirt adhesion aids bed leveling and filament flow.

Shell Thickness or Wall Thickness: Determines outer wall/shell thickness. Affects strength, durability, and surface finish. Thicker walls for strength but may increase printing time and material usage.
『 ☆ Rule Of Thumb for 3D Printing ☆ 』

Proper Bed Levelling: Ensure level bed for adhesion and prevent warping.

Optimal Layer Height: Use appropriate layer height (50-75% of nozzle diameter).

Print Speed: Balance speed for time and quality based on filament and performance.

Wall Thickness and Infill: Ensure sufficient wall thickness (1-1.5 mm) and adjust infill density for strength.

Cooling: Enable cooling for small or intricate features to reduce overheating and improve quality.

Support Structures: Use supports for complex or overhanging parts to maintain accuracy.

Filament Handling: Store filament properly and use guides for consistent feeding.

Calibration and Iteration: Regularly calibrate printer and fine-tune settings for optimal quality and performance.
『 ☆ Creating and 3D Printing a Chess Piece ☆ 』

  1. Launch Fusion 360 and Create a New Design

    Switch to the "Model" workspace.

  2. Import the Picture of the Pawn

    1. In the toolbar, click on the "Canvas" button under the "Insert" menu.

    2. In the canvas dialog box, select the plane where you want to import the picture. For example, you can choose the "Top" plane.

    3. Click on the folder icon next to the "Image" field to browse and select the picture of the pawn you want to import. Make sure the image is in a common format like JPEG or PNG.

    4. Adjust the size and position of the canvas by dragging the corner handles and center point, respectively. Ensure the picture of the pawn is aligned properly.

    5. Click "OK" to import the canvas.

  3. Trace the Outline of the Pawn

    1. Switch to the "Create" menu and choose the appropriate sketch tool to trace the outline of the chess pawn. For instance, you can use the "Line," "Arc," or "Spline" tool.

    2. Start by creating a sketch point at the center of the pawn's base. Then, draw lines, arcs, or splines to recreate the pawn's shape, following the contours of the imported image.

    3. Once you've completed the sketch, use the appropriate sketch tools to apply constraints and dimensions to accurately define the pawn's shape.

    4. Exit the sketch by clicking the "Finish Sketch" button.

  4. Revolve the Sketch

    1. Now, you can extrude the sketch using revolve to give it depth and turn it into a 3D model. Select the sketch and click on the "Create" menu, then choose "Revolve."

    2. In the revolve dialog box, enter the desired plane for the pawn to wrap around to create a full model and set any other parameters as needed. Click "OK" to confirm the extrusion.

  5. Hollow out the Pawn

    1. Now, use the "Shell" feature to hollow out the pawn. Select the model and click on the "Create" menu, then choose "Shell."

    2. In the shell dialog box, enter the desired wall thickness (2mm) and ensure the "Hollow" option is selected. Click "OK" to apply the shell feature.

You should now have a hollow chess pawn model created from the imported picture, with the specified dimensions and wall thickness.
『 ☆ Assignment 2: Handphone Stand ☆ 』

Creating a Custom Phone Stand with the Deathly Hallows Symbol

For this phone stand, I wanted to customize it with a unique design, which was the Deathly Hallows symbol from Harry Potter. I aimed to combine it in my final design while including the minimum requirements and a charger dock.

Designing the Phone Stand

Once the design was finalized, I used Fusion360 and the appropriate sketch tools (lines, arcs, etc.) to draw the Deathly Hallows symbol. I had the option to sketch it freehand or import an image of the symbol as a reference and trace over it. To refine the sketch, I employed additional sketch tools like offset and trim.

To ensure the phone stand fit a standard phone, I extruded the sketch to the desired thickness. The "Extrude" command options allowed me to specify the thickness accurately.

After creating the 3D model, I carefully reviewed it, making necessary adjustments and refinements for an optimal design.

Exporting the Design for 3D Printing

Once satisfied with the design, I proceeded to export the file for 3D printing. In the "File" menu, I selected "Export" > "3D Print" and chose STL as the file format. By clicking "OK," I successfully exported the STL file.

Preparing the STL File for Printing

Next, I needed to prepare the STL file for printing. Here are the settings I used:

  • Temperature: 210°C
  • Velocity: 25
  • Layer height: 0.15mm

The estimated printing time for the phone stand was approximately 5 hours.

Printing and Testing the Phone Stand

After setting up the printing parameters, I initiated the printing process and let it complete.

Once printed, I tested the phone stand by ensuring that my phone fit comfortably in it. I found that the phone could be placed in multiple orientations, allowing for versatile use.

In addition to the fit, I verified that the phone stand enabled charging for optimized functionality.
